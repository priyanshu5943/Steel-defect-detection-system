The ongoing AI revolution has the potential to transform society far beyond its current applications in science and technology. A key driver of this revolution is machine learning (ML), for which increasingly sophisticated methods have been developed. This progress has led to the expectation that machines may outperform humans in most tasks, including intellectual ones, within a few decades. The revolution is driven by two converging movements. The first movement is referred to as "data-intensive discovery," "e-Science," or "big data." This movement involves transforming massive amounts of data into knowledge using various computational methods, including ML. The data is transitioning from a passive role to an active role, which means it is not only used to confirm or refute a hypothesis but also to assist in raising new hypotheses to be tested on an unprecedented scale. The second movement involves natural language processing tools, which are under development to process spoken and written text. Significant progress has been made recently in combining ML and big data to achieve impressive results in speech processing and machine translation. Although computational systems are still far from human-level text interpretation, the synergistic use of big data and ML allows for the creation of intelligent systems capable of handling massive amounts of data with analytical ability. Such systems could generate knowledge without human intervention, which could have a significant impact on society. Despite the optimism surrounding big data and ML, their contributions to society are already significant. ML algorithms can operate on small datasets and require limited computational resources, while working with big data requires proper infrastructure and significant computational power. The goals of ML fall into two distinct types: classification of data instances and making inferences based on the organization and/or structuring of the data. Although considerable work has been devoted to addressing the challenges that arise when materials science meets big data and ML, there is still much to be achieved in the next few decades. In this review paper, we discuss two fundamental areas in Materials Science: ML-based discovery of new materials and ML-based analysis of chemical sensing compounds.
